Bio

I got my B.Sc in Economics from Ajou University as an outstanding student in the top 10% of the major GPA. During this period, I was fascinated by the way economists view the world mathematically, and thus became interested in mathematical modeling and statistical analysis.

In February 2016, I enrolled in the master's program in the Department of Industrial Engineering at Ulsan National Institute of Science and Technology (UNIST) to develop engineering capabilities required for data-driven research. For the first one and a half years, I conducted research to mathematically model the dynamics of two-sided markets from a game-theoretic perspective and to verify them statistically. After then, I took a break from my studies and worked as a data analyst intern at Hyundai Mipo Dockyard for half a year. During an internship, I was engaged in developing text-mining algorithms to automate documentation tasks and to analyze business insights. Completing the internship, I returned to UNIST, transferred to the combined master-Ph.D. course, and joined the lab of Prof.Chiehyeon Lim.

I spent the first two years of Ph.D. course (Jan 2018 - Feb 2020) solving real-world problems with advanced text-mining techniques. For example, I analyzed the development trends of the 4th industrial revolution and blockchain technology using topic modeling such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation or Non-negative Matrix Factorization, and developed a context-aware recommender system with the leverage of Word2Vec. These two studies gave me a deep understanding of Bayesian approach (e.g, Variational Inference), numerical method, and sequential modeling.

For the last three years (Feb 2020 - Dec 2022), I've been focused on developing controllable sequence models, or controllable language models (CLMs), not only because I think it will be increasingly important to control AI models from an end-user perspective in the future, but also because I believe that when AI is fully integrated into our lives, it can be devastating if it gets out of control. In this context, my research goal was materialized as a study of how to combine reinforcement learning (RL) and natural language generation (NLG) methods. I have conducted several studies at the intersection of RL and NLG, from application to theoretical aspects. Some studies were successfully published in top-tier conferences (e.g., KDD'21, NeurIPS'21, KDD'22), and the others are under review (e.g., EMNLP'23) or to be submitted (e.g., AAAI'24, ICLR'24).

In February 2023, I completed my Ph.D. in Industrial Engineering and won the best researcher award. I wrote my Ph.D dissertation on controllable sequence generation, titled "Tackling Three Problems in Controlled Sequence Generations: Bridging Reinforcement Learning with Language Models."   I'm joining the Graduate School of Artificial Intelligence at UNIST as a Postdoc researcher from March 2023. Currently, I belong to the Service Intelligence Lab that is led by Prof.Chiehyeon Lim, my advisor whom I truly respect.

I dream of becoming a researcher who is willing to stand up against prejudice and inequality in the world with logical thinking and a scientific attitude. I am looking forward to a heart-pounding journey of research that will create a better world and promote the future with great colleagues. I will end this article by introducing a quote from one of my favorite scholars, Alfred Marshall:

"Cool head, but warm heart!"

Research interest

Figure.1 (Application Examples of CSG - diet planning (left) and drug repurposing (right)): The data of interest is defined in a sequence format (e.g., diet sequence and compound sequence). The original sequence (i.e., a picture with the caption "before" and "base" in diet planning and drug repurposing examples, respectively) is edited into the desirable sequence whose target property (i.e., color of diet and indication of drug) is controlled as intended.

My research interest is to develop controllable language models (CLMs) for controllable sequence generation (CSG). In general, I implement CLMs by treating the (large) langugage models as agents and leveraging policy-based RL (e.g., REINFORCE, TRPO, PPO, etc.,) to fine-tune them with human-instruction rewards. For the last two years of Ph.D., I have published several papers proposing novel CLMs that specializes in real-world applications, including diet planning [KDD'21, NeurIPS'21], operational recommendations [KDD'22], and drug repurposing [working paper].

Figure.2 (Dual-objective Nature of CLMs (far-left) and How to achieve Pareto Improvement (far-right)): The Pareto frontier is a boundary within the objective space where the set of optimal policies Ï€* belongs to. Each point represents a combination of objectives achieved by the policy at that point. The red arrow indicates that we cannot increase reward without sacrificing likelihood, while the blue arrow indicates vice versa. The purple arrow and orange area denote potential direction and region, respectively, that improves both objectives simultaneously.

More recently, my research focus has shifted toward establishing the theoretical foundations of CLM. This is important because understanding the theoretical basis for ensuring the controllability of AI models can minimize the risks that can arise from rule-of-thumb (i.e., unprincipled) practices for out-of-control situations, which can lead to catastrophic losses amounting to hundreds of billions of dollars in many sectors, e.g., finance, medical, security, transportation, etc.

Figure 2 describes my approach to CLMs from a theoretical perspective. At the core of my approach is the assumption that "developing controllable AI is equivalent to solving multi-objective optimization." To be specific, controlling an AI model requires it to approximate real data distribution (i.e., likelihood objective) and obey user-intended directions (i.e., control objective, or reward objective) at the same time. In other words, developing CLMs is a problem of searching for an optimal solution where both likelihood and reward objectives are satisfied. Accordingly, I hypothesized that the optimal solutions lie on the Pareto frontier, as is typical for multi-objective optimization problems, and that it is inevitable to sacrifice the likelihood for further increasing the reward. These two hypotheses establish the theoretical foundation for the controllability of AI models, which is a unique aspect that distinguishes my approach from previous studies. You can see my work in recent papers that are under review and in progress.
