Bio

I got my B.Sc in Economics from Ajou University as an outstanding student in the top 10% of the major GPA. During this period, I was fascinated by the way economists view the world mathematically, and thus became interested in mathematical modeling and statistical analysis.

In February 2016, I enrolled in the master's program in the Department of Industrial Engineering at Ulsan National Institute of Science and Technology (UNIST) to develop engineering capabilities required for data-driven research. For the first one and a half years, I conducted research to mathematically model the dynamics of two-sided markets from a game-theoretic perspective and to verify them statistically. After then, I took a break from my studies and worked as a data analyst intern at Hyundai Mipo Dockyard for half a year. During an internship, I was engaged in developing text-mining algorithms to automate documentation tasks and to analyze business insights. Completing the internship, I returned to UNIST, transferred to the combined master-Ph.D. course, and joined the lab of Prof.Chiehyeon Lim.

I spent the first two years of Ph.D. course (Jan 2018 - Feb 2020) solving real-world problems with advanced text-mining techniques. For example, I analyzed the development trends of the 4th industrial revolution and blockchain technology using topic modeling such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (Blei et al. (2003)) or Non-negative Matrix Factorization (Lee, D., & Seung, H. S. (2000)), and developed a context-aware recommender system with the leverage of Word2Vec (Mikolov et al. (2013)). These two studies gave me a deep understanding of Bayesian approach (e.g, Variational Inference), numerical method, and sequential modeling.

For the last three years (Feb 2020 - Dec 2022), I've been focused on developing controllable sequence models, or controllable language models (CLMs), not only because I think it will be increasingly important to control AI models from an end-user perspective in the future, but also because I believe that when AI is fully integrated into our lives, it can be devastating if it gets out of control. In this context, my research goal was materialized as a study of how to combine reinforcement learning (RL) and natural language generation (NLG) methods. I have conducted several studies at the intersection of RL and NLG, from application to theoretical aspects. Some studies were successfully published in top-tier conferences (e.g., KDD'21, NeurIPS'21, KDD'22), and the others are under review (e.g., EMNLP'23) or to be submitted (e.g., AAAI'24, ICLR'24).

In February 2023, I completed my Ph.D. in Industrial Engineering and won the best researcher award. I wrote my Ph.D dissertation on controllable sequence generation, titled "Tackling Three Problems in Controlled Sequence Generations: Bridging Reinforcement Learning with Language Models."   I'm joining the Graduate School of Artificial Intelligence at UNIST as a Postdoc researcher from March 2023. Currently, I belong to the Service Intelligence Lab that is led by Prof.Chiehyeon Lim, my advisor whom I truly respect.

I dream of becoming a researcher who is willing to stand up against prejudice and inequality in the world with logical thinking and a scientific attitude. I am looking forward to a heart-pounding journey of research that will create a better world and promote the future with great colleagues. I will end this article by introducing a quote from one of my favorite scholars, Alfred Marshall:

"Cool head, but warm heart!"

Research interest

My research interest is to develop a model for controllable sequence generation (CSG). Specifically, I focus on developing controllable language models (CLMs) where the (large) langugage model is regarded as an agent and finetuned to human-instruction rewards by policy-based RL (e.g., REINFORCE, PPO, TRPO, etc.,). For the last two years of Ph.D., I have published several papers proposing novel CLMs that specializes in real-world problems , including diet planning [KDD'21, NeurIPS'21], operational recommendations [KDD'22], and drug repositioning [working paper].

Recently, my research interest has shifted toward establishing the theoretical foundations of CLM.

This research area is dissected into three elements: 1) sequential model, 2) control theory, and 3) generative framework. Therefore, the three elements must be considered in CSG research, and I usually take them into account according to the three steps as follows:

  1. Building the langauge models of high capacities to estimate sequential patterns (e.g., Seq2Seq, Transformers, GPT3, etc.,).
  2. Leveraging policy-based RL algorithms to induce controllability (e.g., REINFORCE, PPO, TRPO, etc.,).
  3. Optimizing the language models to be controllable using RL algorithms under the generative framework of unsupervised or self-supervised manners (e.g., AE, VAE, etc.,).

As mentioned above, my research boundary is grounded on the middle territory between NLG and RL. On this background, my research interest in CSGs is recently extending from real-world applications to theoretical understanding. Specifically, I study the theoretical foundations of how RL and NLP techniques get intertwined and fused on the lens of Bayesian perspectives. There are four reasons why I prefer the Bayesian approach:

  1. Bayesian methods are one of the most straightforward tools for dealing with conditional distributions.
    • RL is a learning framework that estimates the conditional distribution over actions given states according to the Markov decision process.
    • Language model describes a sequence by factorizing the joint distribution of words into the product of conditional distributions of tokens given previous tokens.
  2. Bayesian methods provide us with a powerful backdoor, called Variational Bayes, to approximate intractable distributions difficult to estimate directly.
    • Variational inference (VI), a mathematical framework for (approximately) doing Bayesian inference, is even computationally cheap because it can be converted to a dual problem, i.e., maximizing ELBO.
  3. Bayesian methods imply the probabilistic status of unobserved states, and it means implicit data augmentation comes into play in the latent space.
    • RL requires as many observations as possible to estimate a policy that maximizes the reward.
  4. Bayesian methods are open to introducing tricks such as marginalization so that we can easily derive useful properties such as the amount of information.
A paper titled " Theoretical Principles of Controllable Generation ," which deals with some of the topics related to the above four contents, was submitted to ICML'23, and is currently in the process of review. Recently, I am thinking about how to introduce the concept of augmentation into CSG research in the causal perspective through the Bayesian method.
